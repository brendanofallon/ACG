The basic idea

ACG is a tool that allows genetics researchers to infer a wide variety of parameters about a population from genetic data. As input, ACG takes an alignment of genetic sequences - that is, DNA sequences from the same genomic region sampled from multiple individuals. The software then tries to construct on evolutionary model that best fits the sequence data. The evolutionary model contains many aspects of the history of the population including its (potentially time-dependent) size, the genealogical tree of the samples, the recombination rate, and how likely individual nucleotides are to mutate to other nucleotides. Many aspects of the model are configurable by the researcher - for instance, you can tell ACG that you think the population has been exponentially growing over time and to try to infer the growth rate and current size.

ACG accomplishes the above task by constructing and running a Markov chain that, over time, samples states in proportion to their likelihood given the evolutionary model you supplied. This is a random process, and ACG slowly homes in on likely states over time. As your run progresses you can easily monitor that states being sampled by clicking on the "Model State" tab and watching the values of the parameters change over time. Once you've collected enough samples (a few tens of thousands should do) you'll have a pretty good idea of what the population size is, or where the recombination breakpoints are, or what the evolutionary tree looks like at a certain site.

Of trees and ARGs

ACG is able to accomplish its task by imagining the history of the sequences as an Ancestral Recombination Graph, or ARG. An ARG is a bit like a stack of simple (non-recombining) trees, where recombination points separate one tree from the next. When just a few recombination breakpoints are present, ARGs are relatively simple to visualize. But when there are many samples and many recombinations its quite difficult to get a good handle on the whole thing at once. ACG addresses this by allowing researchers to look at the trees that exist at individual sites.
